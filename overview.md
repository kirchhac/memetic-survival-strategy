This is a fascinating theoretical framework that attempts to connect thermodynamics, astronomy, and the divergence of Eastern and Western philosophical traditions. Let me engage critically with each component while exploring both its strengths and potential issues:

On the Thermodynamic Foundation (Points 1-2)
Your connection between life, entropy reduction, and prediction is intriguing and has some scientific basis. Living systems do create local order (reducing entropy locally) while increasing total universal entropy. The predictive processing framework in neuroscience does suggest our brains are fundamentally prediction machines. However, I'd note that "minimizing environmental entropy" might be too broad - organisms more specifically maintain their own internal order while often increasing environmental entropy.

On Astronomy as Fundamental (Points 3-5)
The claim that astronomical knowledge was essential for survival is well-supported archaeologically. However, the universality you suggest needs qualification. Different cultures developed very different astronomical systems - some focused heavily on lunar cycles, others on Venus, some on different star patterns entirely. The encoding in myths is documented, but the specific patterns varied enormously.

On Time and Calendars (Point 6)
While calendars were certainly crucial innovations, calling them humanity's "greatest accomplishment" is debatable. Also, your timeline conflates different developments - mechanical clocks emerged in medieval Europe, but many other civilizations had sophisticated time-keeping without them. The Church's control of time in medieval Europe was indeed significant, but this was a specifically Western European phenomenon, not universal.

Critical Issues with the Equatorial vs. Ecliptic Distinction (Points 7-9)
This is where your hypothesis becomes most speculative and problematic:

Empirical problems:

Ancient Chinese astronomy was extremely sophisticated regarding the ecliptic. They tracked planetary movements meticulously, had zodiacal systems, and recorded eclipses for millennia
Indian (Vedic) astronomy also extensively used the ecliptic, with detailed planetary calculations
The Maya had incredibly precise knowledge of Venus's path along the ecliptic
Japanese, Korean, and Islamic astronomical traditions all incorporated ecliptic observations
Death-resurrection myths aren't uniquely Western:

Hindu cosmology includes cycles of death and rebirth (Shiva as destroyer/creator)
Buddhist concepts of rebirth and renewal
Chinese myths of renewal (Phoenix mythology)
The distinction isn't as clear-cut as you suggest
The individualism connection is tenuous:

Astrological birth charts exist in both Indian (Jyotish) and Chinese (BaZi) traditions
The link between ecliptic observation and individualistic philosophy lacks clear causal mechanism
Many ecliptic-observing cultures (Babylonian, Egyptian) weren't notably individualistic
Alternative Explanations
Rather than astronomical differences, East-West philosophical divergence might better be explained by:

Agricultural systems: Rice cultivation (requiring collective irrigation) vs. wheat cultivation (more individual)
Geography: River valley civilizations vs. Mediterranean maritime cultures
Political development: Early bureaucratic states (China) vs. city-states (Greece)
Historical contingency: Specific thinkers and schools that happened to gain influence
Trade patterns: Different networks of cultural exchange
The Deeper Issue
Your framework assumes that astronomical observation methods caused philosophical differences, but the causation could easily run the other way - different philosophical orientations might have led to emphasizing different astronomical phenomena. Or both could be effects of other causes entirely.

The hypothesis also risks oversimplifying "Eastern" and "Western" thought into monolithic categories, when both traditions contain enormous internal diversity. Stoicism shares much with Daoism; Mohism was quite individualistic; Epicureanism emphasized withdrawal from public life similar to some Eastern philosophies.

While your synthesis is creative and thought-provoking, it needs more careful attention to the historical record and the complexity of causal relationships between astronomy, philosophy, and culture. The actual development of these traditions was likely far messier and more contingent than this elegant thermodynamic-astronomical determinism suggests.
